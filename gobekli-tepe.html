Behind the Science · The Shared Foundation

Göbekli Tepe
& Karahantepe

Twelve thousand years old. Deliberately buried by the people who built them. Neither site is fully excavated. Neither site is fully explained.

Southeastern Turkey  ·  c. 9600–8000 BCE  ·  UNESCO World Heritage Site

The Hook

Everything you learned about when human civilization began is probably wrong. Göbekli Tepe is the reason why.

Here is what we thought we knew: humans were nomadic hunter-gatherers until about 10,000 years ago, when they discovered agriculture, settled down, built villages, and eventually got organized enough to construct large buildings and temples. Civilization came first, then the monuments.

Göbekli Tepe — a site in southeastern Turkey near the city of Şanlıurfa — destroyed that timeline. The structures there are 12,000 years old. That is 7,000 years before Stonehenge. Six thousand years before the pyramids at Giza. Built at a time when, according to everything we thought we understood, humans were still living in small bands hunting animals and gathering plants. They had no writing. No metal tools. No pottery. No domesticated work animals. No wheels.

And yet they organized the labor of hundreds — possibly thousands — of people to quarry, shape, and transport limestone pillars weighing up to 16 tons, arrange them in precise circles, and carve them with an extraordinary density of animal imagery: foxes, scorpions, vultures, snakes, wild boars, cranes. The carving is not primitive. It is accomplished. The iconography is consistent and deliberate.

"Göbekli Tepe is the oldest known example of monumental architecture — the first structure human beings put together that was bigger and more complicated than a hut. When these pillars were erected, so far as we know, nothing of comparable scale existed in the world."

National Geographic

The Basic Facts
AgeApproximately 11,600–12,000 years old. The oldest structures date to roughly 9600 BCE.
LocationŞanlıurfa province, southeastern Turkey. On a limestone ridge approximately 15 km northeast of the city of Şanlıurfa.
ScaleAt least 20 circular enclosures confirmed. Over 200 T-shaped pillars documented. Less than 10% of the site has been excavated.
The pillarsT-shaped limestone columns up to 18 feet (5.5 meters) tall, weighing up to 16 tons. Carved with intricate bas-reliefs of animals and abstract symbols.
The buildersHunter-gatherers. Pre-agricultural. Pre-pottery. Pre-writing. Operating without metal tools or domesticated work animals.
StatusUNESCO World Heritage Site since 2018. Ongoing excavation under the Turkish Ministry of Culture and Tourism.
The Detail That Changes Everything
Why It Matters

The discovery of Göbekli Tepe in the 1990s — led by German archaeologist Klaus Schmidt, who dedicated the rest of his life to the site — forced a fundamental rethinking of what drives civilization. The old model said agriculture came first, then settlement, then organization, then monuments. Göbekli Tepe reversed the sequence.

Building something on this scale required people to gather in large numbers, coordinate effort over long periods, and feed a substantial workforce. Some researchers now believe the demand for grain to feed the builders of Göbekli Tepe may itself have been a driver of early agriculture — that the monument came first, and farming developed to sustain the people working on it. Religion, or at least organized communal purpose, may have preceded and created civilization rather than being produced by it.

Then there is the other detail — the one that nobody has adequately explained. Around 8,000 BCE, approximately 2,000 years after the site was built, someone deliberately buried it. Not abandoned it — buried it. Filled it in. Covered the pillars with tons of rubble and soil and walked away. The burial was so thorough and so deliberate that the site was preserved nearly intact beneath the surface for ten thousand years, until a Kurdish shepherd noticed an unusual concentration of flint tools on a hillside in 1994.

Who buried it? Why? The site contains no graves, no domestic refuse, no evidence of permanent habitation. It was not a village. It was something people came to — from considerable distances, evidence suggests — and then left. The purpose of the structures, the meaning of the carvings, and the reason for the deliberate burial remain genuinely, completely unknown.

The Sister Site
Karahantepe

Forty-six kilometers to the east of Göbekli Tepe, excavation work begun in earnest in 2019 has revealed a sister site that in some ways is even more striking. Karahantepe covers approximately 14 hectares — larger than Göbekli Tepe — and dates to roughly the same period, with radiocarbon analyses placing its earliest occupation at around 9,400 BCE.

What makes Karahantepe different is the human imagery. Where Göbekli Tepe is dominated by animal carvings, Karahantepe is full of human faces, human figures, and human-animal hybrids. In 2023, excavators uncovered a 2.3-meter-tall statue — one of the earliest known realistic human depictions in the world — fixed in a seated position on a stone bench. That same season they found the world's oldest known painted statue: a life-size wild boar in limestone, still bearing traces of red, white, and black pigment on its surface.

Most recently, in late 2025, archaeologists uncovered what appears to be a Neolithic amphitheater — a 17-meter-wide circular structure with tiered stone benches, carved heads embedded in the walls, and a central space that appears designed for communal gathering. The structure was in active use for several centuries. Evidence from the site now includes traces of early cereal cultivation, suggesting that the shift to agriculture was happening at and around these sites — not in spite of them.

Both Göbekli Tepe and Karahantepe are part of the Turkish government's Taş Tepeler ("Stone Hills") project, which has now identified twelve related sites across the same region, all dating to the same cultural horizon. This is not an isolated anomaly. It is evidence of a widespread, organized, symbolically sophisticated culture operating in the Neolithic period at a scale and complexity we are only beginning to understand.

Pillar 43 · The Vulture Stone

The most studied single object at Göbekli Tepe. The entire western face of Pillar 43 in Enclosure D is covered with carved imagery: a large vulture with one wing raised, balancing a sphere or disc; a headless human figure; three rectangular objects sometimes interpreted as architectural structures; an ibis; a scorpion; a fox. Researchers have proposed that the imagery encodes a specific astronomical date — possibly 10,825 BCE — making it potentially the world's oldest calendar. The meaning of the "handbag" symbols at the top of the pillar remains genuinely disputed. They appear at other sites across the ancient world.

In The Recurrence Protocol, it is a specific scoring pattern on a disc found near Pillar 43 that gives Daniel the mathematical key he needs — not the obvious imagery everyone else has been studying, but a secondary inscription that requires someone who knows what they're looking for.

What This Means in the Books

In The Recurrence Protocol, Karahantepe is not a temple. It is a transmitter. The T-shaped pillars — with their specific astronomical alignments, their placement on geology with unusually strong piezoelectric properties, and the precision of their carving — are components of a planetary-scale signaling system built by a civilization operating 12,000 years ago with a knowledge of physics and astronomy that we are only now beginning to reverse-engineer.

The deliberate burial is not abandonment. It is preservation. The people who built Karahantepe buried it because they knew what it was for, they knew when it would be needed again, and they understood that the only way to protect it across twelve millennia was to hide it in plain sight — as a hill, as soil, as an archaeological mystery waiting for someone to ask the right questions at the right time.

The fictional Daniel Mercer asks those questions. The real Miriam Hale — the archaeologist at the excavation — has been there for three seasons by the time Daniel contacts her. She replied to his message within minutes, which tells Daniel everything he needs to know: she has been waiting for someone to ask.
